The Ultimate Guide To Learning How To Fish

Fishing requires a few essential pieces of gear. Knowing what you need is the first step to a successful fishing trip. To get started, you need to choose what reel, rod, and tackle to use. Additionally, it is important to understand the environment you will be fishing in, the type of fish you are targeting, and the techniques best suited for both. Whether you are a beginner or looking to refine your skills, having the right knowledge and equipment will enhance your fishing experience and increase your chances of success.

In this guide, we will cover the basics of fishing gear, including the different types of reels, rods, and tackle. We will also discuss the various baits and lures you can use, as well as some fundamental tips and techniques to get you started. With the right preparation and a bit of practice, you will be well on your way to becoming a proficient angler. Let's dive in and explore the essential components of fishing gear to help you make informed choices and enjoy your time on the water.

Fishing Reels

Spinning Reel

Spinning reels, also known as fixed spool reels, are among the most popular types of fishing reels. They are mounted below the rod and are good for beginners due to their ease of use. The open-faced design allows the fishing line to flow freely off the spool, making it easier to cast lightweight lures and baits.

What It's For:

Spinning reels are ideal for light tackle fishing and using live bait. They work well for freshwater fishing, such as trout and bass fishing, and can also be effective in saltwater environments.

Baitcaster Reel

Baitcasting reels are more advanced and offer better control and accuracy. They sit atop the fishing rod with the spool parallel to the rod. Casting with a baitcaster involves controlling the spool with the thumb to avoid tangles and bird nests.

What It's For:

Baitcasting reels are preferred by experienced anglers targeting larger freshwater or saltwater species. They are excellent for precise lure placements and are commonly used for bass fishing and other predatory fish due to their strength and precision.

Fly Reel

Fly reels are uniquely designed for fly fishing. They are manual reels that require the angler to pull the line with one hand and cast the rod with the other. Unlike other reels, the primary purpose of a fly reel is to store line, provide smooth uninterrupted tension, and manage line retrieval.

What It's For:

Fly reels are used exclusively for fly fishing, which targets fish like trout and salmon. They are effective in both freshwater and saltwater environments and are essential for presenting the fly naturally.

What Should You Use?

A good starter reel is typically a spinning reel, which is easy to handle and versatile enough for beginners. Spinning reels are also tough and durable, perfect for a beginner's first reel.

Fishing Rods

Spinning Rods

Spinning rods are the most versatile and widely used type of fishing rod. They are suitable for a range of fishing environments, from freshwater to saltwater. Spinning rods are designed to hold spinning reels and are ideal for beginners due to their ease of use.

Characteristics:

  • The reel is mounted underneath the rod.
  • Suitable for light to medium tackle.
  • Excellent for casting lightweight lures and baits.
  • Commonly used for freshwater species like trout and bass, as well as saltwater species.

Baitcasting Rods

Baitcasting rods are used with baitcasting reels and offer greater control and accuracy compared to spinning rods. They are preferred by more experienced anglers and are often used for targeting larger fish.

Characteristics:

  • The reel is mounted on top of the rod.
  • Suitable for medium to heavy tackle.
  • Allows for precise casting and control over the lure.
  • Commonly used for bass fishing and other large freshwater and saltwater species.

Fly Rods

Fly rods are specifically designed for fly fishing. They are used with fly reels and fly lines to cast lightweight flies with precision. Fly fishing is a specialized technique that requires skill and practice.

Characteristics:

  • Long and flexible rods designed for casting lightweight flies.
  • Used with a fly reel and fly line.
  • Suitable for both freshwater and saltwater environments.
  • Commonly used for trout, salmon, and other species that respond well to fly fishing techniques.

What Should You Use?

Fishing rods come in various sizes and styles, each suited to different types of fishing. A good starter rod is a spinning rod paired with a spinning reel. A spinning rod and reel will allow beginners to experience as many types of fishing as possible.

Fishing Tackle

Sinkers

Sinkers are weights used in fishing to sink the bait or lure to the desired depth. They help maintain the bait's position in the water and can be used in various fishing techniques. Additionally, having a sinker on your fishing line increases the casting distance and prevents the bait from drifting with the current.

Types Of Sinkers:

  • Split Shot Sinkers: Small, round weights that can be pinched onto the fishing line. Ideal for light tackle and fine-tuning depth.
  • Egg Sinkers: Oval-shaped weights with a hole through the center. They allow the line to pass through, providing a natural presentation.
  • Bullet Sinkers: Shaped like a bullet, commonly used in Texas rigs for bass fishing.
  • Pyramid Sinkers: Triangular-shaped weights that hold well in sandy or muddy bottoms. Often used in surf fishing.
  • Bank Sinkers: Tear-drop shaped weights with a loop at the top. They are versatile and used in various fishing situations.

Leaders

Leaders are lengths of line attached between the main fishing line and the hook or lure. They provide additional strength, abrasion resistance, and invisibility in the water. Additionally, leaders are needed to absorb the shock of aggressive strikes from fishes and reduce the visibility of the line near the bait.

Types Of Leaders:

  • Monofilament Leaders: Made from a single strand of nylon. They are flexible, transparent, and provide some stretch.
  • Fluorocarbon Leaders: Nearly invisible underwater, with higher abrasion resistance and less stretch than monofilament.
  • Wire Leaders: Made from steel or titanium wire. They are essential for toothy fish like pike or sharks to prevent bite-offs.

Floats

Floats, also known as bobbers, are buoyant devices attached to the fishing line to suspend the bait at a specific depth and indicate when a fish bites. Additionally, floats provide visual indication of a fish biting.

Types Of Floats:

  • Fixed Floats: Attached at a fixed point on the line. Suitable for shallow waters.
  • Slip Floats: Slide along the line, allowing for adjustable depth settings. Used for deeper waters.
  • Bubble Floats: Clear plastic floats that can be filled with water to add weight for casting.

Hooks

Hooks are essential components that secure the bait and catch the fish. They come in various shapes, sizes, and styles to suit different fishing techniques and target species.

Types Of Hooks:

  • J-Hooks: Traditional hook shape with a curved shank and a pointed tip. Suitable for most types of fishing.
  • Circle Hooks: Designed with a circular bend, reducing the chances of deep hooking and improving fish survival rates upon release.
  • Treble Hooks: Feature three hooks joined at a single shank. Commonly used on lures for increased hook-up rates.
  • Octopus Hooks: Short-shank hooks with a wide gap, ideal for live bait fishing.
  • Bait Hooks: Hooks with barbs along the shank to hold bait securely.

What Should You Use?

Fishing hooks depend on the type of fishing and bait used. However, beginners should opt for J-Hooks as they are versatile hooks that can be used for different fishing methods.

Bait

Natural Bait

Natural bait includes live or dead organisms that fish naturally feed on. This type of bait is often more effective because it is part of the fish's natural diet.

Types Of Natural Bait:

  • Nightcrawlers: Large earthworms used for freshwater fishing, effective for bass, trout, and catfish.
  • Shiners: Small fish used for targeting larger freshwater fish like bass and pike.
  • Fish: Cut bait are pieces of fish, such as herring or mackerel, used for catfish and other predatory fish. Alternatively, live whole fish can be used to catch larger predatory fishes.
  • Shrimp: Used in both freshwater and saltwater fishing, effective for species like redfish, snapper, and trout.
  • Clams and Mussels: Used for freshwater species like carp and catfish.

Artificial Bait

Artificial bait, also known as lures, is designed to mimic the appearance and movement of natural prey. These baits come in various shapes, sizes, and colors to attract different types of fish.

Types Of Artificial Bait:

  • Soft Plastics: Versatile and widely used in various fishing techniques, designed to mimic worms, grubs, and small fish.
  • Hard Lures: Made from materials like plastic or wood, designed to mimic the appearance and movement of fish, insects, and other prey.
  • Topwater Lures: Float on the surface and create commotion to attract fish, used to provoke aggressive strikes from fish feeding near the surface.
  • Flies: Specially designed lures used in fly fishing, tied with materials like feathers, fur, and synthetic fibers to imitate insects, baitfish, or other prey.

What Should You Use?

The type of bait you should use depends on the type of fishing and target species. However, beginners should opt for natural baits as there is less work needed compared to artificial bait.
